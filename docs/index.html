Introduction

Technical indicators like KDJ and MACD are widely used by traders to time entries and exits. But do they actually work? And if so, which parameters matter most?

We designed two trading strategies — B1 (oversold rebound) and B2 (momentum confirmation) — and backtested them with rigorous position management across S&P 500 (505 stocks, 2013–2018) and 3,066 Chinese A-share stocks (2015–2026). We then ran a parameter sweep (81 combinations per strategy) to find optimal stop-loss, observation period, and profit-taking thresholds.

Strategy Definitions

B1 — Oversold Rebound

Entry: KDJ J < 13 + MACD DEA > 0 + Uptrend confirmed (white line > yellow line)

Logic: Buy the next day when price is extremely oversold but the macro trend is still intact.

B2 — Momentum Confirmation

Entry: After B1 appears → daily gain ≥ 4% + KDJ J < 55 + volume surge

Logic: A B1 stock that breaks out with volume confirms the reversal.

Position Management

Both strategies use the same exit framework: hard stop-loss (2–4% below entry), observation period (sell if not profitable after N days), fly trigger (sell half when gain ≥ 5–10%), and S1 signal (exit remaining on a high-volume bearish candle). The backtest uses 10 position slots for B1 and 4 slots for B2, with dynamic position sizing based on current portfolio value.

Conclusion

Our cross-market backtest of the B1/B2 oversold rebound strategy reveals several important findings:

  1. Stop-loss is the most important parameter. Across both markets and both strategies, a tight 2% stop-loss dramatically improves risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe +38% vs 4% stop-loss). When trading mean-reversion, wrong entries should be cut immediately.
  2. B1 is cross-market robust. The same strategy with nearly identical optimal parameters works in both S&P 500 (Sharpe 1.42) and A-shares (Sharpe 1.59). This suggests the oversold-rebound pattern is a fundamental market behavior, not a market-specific anomaly.
  3. B2 is market-dependent. B2’s momentum confirmation mechanism works spectacularly in A-shares (+1,143% over 11 years) but underperforms in S&P 500 (+26% over 5 years), likely due to different market microstructure and volatility patterns.
  4. Asymmetric returns drive profitability. Both strategies have low win rates (~30%), but profitable trades generate outsized returns through the “fly” mechanism. The key is preserving capital on losers (tight stop-loss) while letting winners run.
